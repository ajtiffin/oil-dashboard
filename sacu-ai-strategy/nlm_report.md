# Briefing Document: AI Strategy and Regulatory Regimes in the SACU Region

## Executive Summary

The Southern African Customs Union (SACU) region is currently experiencing a rapid shift from high-level digital principles to concrete artificial intelligence (AI) regulatory frameworks. These national efforts are increasingly aligned with the African Union (AU) Continental AI Strategy, approved in July 2024, which provides a unified framework for member states to develop policies that balance socio-economic development with risk mitigation. However, the pace and depth of progress varies enormously across the five SACU member states, creating an uneven and fragmented regulatory landscape that risks undermining the benefits of regional integration.

---

## 1. Current State of AI Policy by Country

### 1.1 South Africa: Sector-Specific Multi-Regulator Model

South Africa is the regional leader in AI readiness, driven by foundational work that began with the 2020 Presidential Commission Report on the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR).

**Key Policies and Dates:**

- **National AI Policy Framework (NAPF):** Released in August 2024, this document established the strategic building blocks for the country's AI ecosystem.
- **Draft National AI Policy:** Submitted to Cabinet in February 2026 and expected to be gazetted for a 60-day public consultation in March 2026. Implementation is projected for 2027/2028.
- **National Data and Cloud Policy:** Gazetted in May 2024, it emphasizes a "cloud-first" approach and data sovereignty to support AI development.

**Strategic Pillars:** The 2026 Draft Policy identifies five core pillars:
1. Skills capacity and talent development
2. Responsible AI governance
3. Ethical and inclusive AI (focused on local datasets)
4. Cultural preservation (digitizing indigenous languages)
5. Human-centered deployment

**Regulatory Approach:** South Africa has opted for a "middle road" approach, which is sector-specific and risk-based. Rather than creating a single centralized AI regulator, oversight is distributed among existing authorities (such as ICASA and the Information Regulator), embedding AI accountability within established frameworks like the Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA).

### 1.2 Botswana: Knowledge-Based Transformation and Data Privacy

Botswana's AI strategy is integrated into its broader goal of becoming a knowledge-based economy by 2036.

- **SmartBots Digital Transformation Strategy:** Adopted in July 2020, this strategy guides digital progress across all sectors and includes general provisions for AI and data science promotion.
- **Data Protection Act 18 of 2024:** Published in October 2024 and became effective on January 14, 2025. It replaced the 2018 Act to better address emerging AI challenges, expanding the powers of the Information and Data Protection Commission and imposing stricter obligations on data processors.
- **Digital Transformation Support:** A three-year EU-funded project (2024–2027) with a budget of €4.85 million is currently supporting the implementation of the SmartBots strategy and the strengthening of legal and governance frameworks.
- Botswana's central bank has already deployed AI chatbots like Proto AICX for multilingual customer assistance.

### 1.3 Namibia: Landmark AI and Digital Safety Legislation

Namibia is currently fast-tracking comprehensive legislation to manage emerging technological risks and modernize its digital governance.

- **Draft AI Bill:** In June 2025, the government announced progress on a landmark Draft AI Bill intended to guide ethical and accountable deployment.
- **Legislative Package:** The AI Bill is being developed alongside a Data Protection Bill and a Cybercrime Bill. Supporting efforts include updates to the Communications Act and the creation of a National AI Strategy.
- **Digital Governance:** Namibia is implementing an e-Parliament Strategy to digitize legislative processes and exploring AI tools like data analytics and virtual research assistants for citizen engagement.
- **International Collaboration:** The country is working with UNESCO through its Readiness Assessment Methodology (RAM) to evaluate its institutional capacity for AI.

### 1.4 Eswatini: Digital Readiness and Fintech Leadership

Eswatini has focused its AI and digital efforts on assessing maturity and establishing a competitive digital finance ecosystem.

- **Digital Readiness Assessment (DRA):** Conducted in late 2024 in collaboration with the UNDP, the assessment scored Eswatini at 2.6 out of 5, placing it in the "systematic" stage of digital maturity. The report identified critical gaps in regulation and infrastructure that lag behind the economy and government pillars.
- **National Fintech Strategy (2025–2030):** Launched on December 4, 2025, this is a blueprint for a well-regulated digital finance ecosystem. Its pillars include agile regulation, human capital, and regional integration.
- **Eswatini Digitalisation Strategy 2024–2028:** This broader agenda includes the "Government in Your Hand" project to transition to integrated nationwide digital services.

### 1.5 Lesotho: Strategic Ecosystem and AI Vision 2035

Lesotho is working to overcome geographic isolation through a collaborative digital ecosystem aimed at sustainable growth.

- **AI Policy:** Lesotho highlighted its newly developed and validated Artificial Intelligence (AI) Policy in March 2025. The policy aligns with the AU Continental Strategy and aims to integrate AI into diverse sectors to enhance innovation.
- **National Digital Transformation Strategy: Agenda 2030:** This framework focuses on five pillars: Enabling Environment; Digital Government; Digital Infrastructure; Digital Population; and Digital Business.
- **Vision 2035:** Lesotho's National Digital Policy 2024 envisions the country as a "digitally empowered society" by 2035.
- **Regulatory Status:** While a vision is in place, Lesotho faces a "lengthy and unpredictable" process of law adoption. The Data Protection Act of 2011 is considered out of date, and the Computer Crime and Security Bill (2023) and Electronic Transactions and Electronic Commerce Bill have faced delays in parliament.

## 2. Elements of a Good AI Regulatory Framework for Developing Countries

A robust framework for developing countries should be locally grounded rather than a "one-size-fits-all" solution, utilizing a mix of self-governance, soft law, hard law, and regulatory sandboxes to reflect unique institutional contexts.

### 2.1 Foundational Infrastructure and Capacity Building
Frameworks must prioritize investments in digital infrastructure, such as high-speed internet, data centers, and a stable power supply. Parallel to this, nations must implement AI literacy programs at all educational levels and vocational training in data science to build a workforce capable of safe and efficient AI deployment.

### 2.2 Ethical Governance and Human Rights
Following UNESCO recommendations, frameworks must institutionalize principles of trust, transparency, and accountability. This includes mandatory ethical impact assessments to identify potential harm and ensures that AI systems are inclusive, fair, and respectful of human dignity.

### 2.3 Data Governance and Sovereignty
Effective regulation requires clear rules on data ownership and protection. Policymakers should establish standards for informed consent and transparency in data usage while developing "local-first" strategies to ensure data generated within a country is leveraged for its own citizens.

### 2.4 Agile and Multi-Stakeholder Approaches
Regulations must be flexible to keep pace with rapid technological evolution. This involves a "whole-of-government" approach, engaging academia, the private sector, and civil society through committees that oversee different sectoral applications of AI, such as in health or finance.

## 3. Benefits of Coordinated AI Governance in the SACU/SADC Region

The African Union (AU) Continental AI Strategy and the SADC Digital Transformation Strategy (DTS) emphasize that regional integration is vital for Africa to become a global player rather than just a consumer of technology.

### 3.1 Regional Sovereignty and Negotiating Power
Harmonized data policies across the SADC region allow African tech companies to compete more effectively at home and abroad. A unified front strengthens the region's negotiating power relative to global technology giants and other international jurisdictions like the EU.

### 3.2 Establishment of a Digital Single Market
Coordinated efforts facilitate cross-border data flows and interoperable digital services, which are essential for the success of the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA). This includes regional standards for digital identity, mobile money, and cybersecurity.

### 3.3 Resource Optimization and Knowledge Sharing
A harmonized approach prevents regulatory fragmentation and allows nations to share best practices, technical expertise, and infrastructure. The SADC DTS targets the creation of regional AI Hubs and centers of excellence to pool talent and funding.

### 3.4 Inclusive Socio-Economic Development
The AU Strategy specifically aims to harness AI for priority sectors like agriculture and healthcare, ensuring that benefits reach marginalized and rural populations through targeted regional initiatives.

## 4. Costs and Risks of Not Having a Coherent AI Regulatory Regime

### 4.1 Economic Risks and Loss of FDI
Investors and the private sector largely seek regulatory certainty to protect their capital. An unstable or absent regulatory environment deters significant investment in digital technologies, data centers, and local startups.

### 4.2 Digital and Algorithmic Colonialism
Without local oversight, multinational firms may extract and exploit African data with little accountability. Furthermore, relying on AI models trained purely on Western datasets can lead to cultural erasure and systemic biases that do not reflect African realities.

### 4.3 Brain Drain
Africa currently accounts for only approximately 3% of global AI talent. Without a supportive local ecosystem and clear career pathways, skilled professionals often migrate to international markets, further depleting the continent's innovative capacity.

### 4.4 Regulatory Arbitrage and Exploitation
A lack of common standards allows for regulatory arbitrage, where companies may deploy high-risk AI applications in jurisdictions with the weakest protections. This can lead to the misuse of personal data and the infringement of human rights, particularly in surveillance or predictive policing.

### 4.5 Deepening the Digital Divide
Unregulated AI risks exacerbating existing socio-economic inequalities. Wealthier communities may gain exclusive access to AI-driven advancements in education and health, while marginalized groups — lacking connectivity or literacy — fall further behind.

### 4.6 Legal Ambiguity and Liability
In countries like South Africa, the lack of dedicated AI legislation creates concerns regarding accountability and liability when AI systems cause harm. Granting AI "legal personhood," as seen in certain patent cases, can create loopholes that allow developers to avoid financial and legal culpability.
