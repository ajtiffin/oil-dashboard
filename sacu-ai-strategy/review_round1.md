# Review: AI Strategy and Regulation in the SACU Region (Round 1)

**Reviewer:** Good-faith critical review
**Date:** 25 March 2026
**Document reviewed:** draft.md

---

## Executive Assessment

This is a well-structured, clearly written policy report that makes a genuinely useful contribution to an under-analyzed topic -- AI governance coordination within SACU. The country-by-country analysis is the report's strongest section, demonstrating careful research and fair characterization of each member's position. However, the report suffers from several factual errors (particularly around the Oxford Insights rankings), an over-reliance on a single contested GDP figure, significant coverage gaps on labour markets, gender, the private sector, and geopolitical dynamics, and recommendations that -- while sensible in direction -- lack the cost estimates and institutional detail needed to be actionable.

---

## Critical Issues (Must-Fix)

### 1. Oxford Insights AI Readiness Index scores appear to be from the wrong edition

The report repeatedly cites the "Oxford Insights Government AI Readiness Index 2025" with the following figures: South Africa 55th (60.57), Botswana 88th (37.10), Namibia 111th (32.62), Eswatini 154th (21.04), Lesotho 168th (17.59). The Editor's Notes claim these are "verified via the 2025 index."

However, web searches indicate the 2025 edition (published December 2025) places South Africa at approximately 67th globally with a score of ~53.27. Mauritius, not South Africa, leads Sub-Saharan Africa with 53.27. The figures cited in the report -- particularly South Africa at 60.57 and rank 55 -- appear to originate from the **2024 edition** of the index. If so, the entire data table in Section 3.1 is mislabeled, and the claim that South Africa is "1st in SACU, top 5 in Africa" under the 2025 index needs verification (Mauritius outranks SA in the 2025 edition).

**Action required:** Download the actual 2025 PDF report from Oxford Insights and verify every ranking and score. Update the table, all in-text references, and the Editor's Notes accordingly. If the 2024 figures are being used, label them honestly as 2024.

### 2. The McKinsey $1.2 trillion GDP figure is misattributed and poorly sourced

The report states: "McKinsey Global Institute estimates that AI could contribute up to $1.2 trillion to Africa's GDP by 2030." The Editor's Notes acknowledge uncertainty but still attribute it to McKinsey. In fact:

- The $1.2 trillion figure appears to originate from **PwC's "Sizing the Prize" framework** and subsequent development agency reports, not McKinsey.
- McKinsey's own "Leading, Not Lagging" report estimates **$61-103 billion annually** from generative AI specifically in Africa -- a substantially different figure.
- A Google executive has cited $1.5 trillion, further muddying the landscape.

The report then compounds the error by using this unverified $1.2 trillion to calculate a SACU-specific estimate of "$50-100 billion over the coming decade" (Section 7.1). This is a back-of-envelope calculation built on a misattributed source, presented without caveats.

**Action required:** Either (a) correctly attribute the $1.2T figure to PwC and note the wide range of estimates, or (b) use the more conservative and properly sourced McKinsey figures. The SACU-specific extrapolation should either be removed or presented with explicit uncertainty bounds and methodological caveats.

### 3. Internal inconsistency on South Africa's GDP share

The Executive Summary states South Africa "accounts for roughly 93% of the bloc's combined GDP." Section 3.1 states "South Africa accounts for 91% of SACU's combined GDP." The report's own data table shows $426.4bn / $467.7bn = 91.2%. The 93% figure in the Executive Summary is wrong by the report's own numbers.

**Action required:** Pick one figure and use it consistently. Based on the data table, 91% is correct.

### 4. Botswana GDP contraction figure is outdated

The report states the IMF projects a GDP contraction of "0.4% in 2025." This was an earlier projection (approximately April 2025). The IMF's September 2025 Article IV consultation revised the contraction to **-1.0% in 2025**, following a **-3.0% contraction in 2024**. The diamond sector's situation is materially worse than the report suggests: mining output fell 24% and diamond trading slumped 34%. The fiscal deficit is expected to exceed 8% of GDP.

**Action required:** Update to the September 2025 IMF Article IV figures and strengthen the analysis of how the diamond crisis constrains Botswana's fiscal capacity for digital investment.

### 5. South Africa unemployment figure understated

The report refers to "South Africa's 32%+ unemployment rate." As of Q4 2025, the official unemployment rate is **31.4%** (down from a peak of 33.2% in Q2 2025). The broader combined rate (including discouraged workers and underemployed) is **42.1%**. The "32%+" framing is misleading -- it understates the severity when the expanded measure is considered and overstates it relative to the latest official figure.

**Action required:** Use the Q4 2025 figure (31.4%) and note the expanded rate (42.1%) for context. This is especially important given AI's labour market implications.

## Substantive Suggestions (Would Significantly Improve the Report)

### 6. Labour market impact is almost entirely absent

This is the most glaring coverage gap. The report mentions unemployment once (in passing) and frames AI as something that should "augment rather than replace human labor." But there is no analysis of:

- Which sectors and occupations in SACU are most exposed to AI-driven automation
- The specific vulnerability of South Africa's large, low-skilled workforce (research shows augmentation-exposed jobs growing ~20% while automation-exposed jobs declining ~2%)
- Youth unemployment (57% for 15-24 year olds in SA) and how AI might worsen or alleviate it
- The implications for the smaller SACU economies where a large share of employment is informal

For a region where unemployment is the defining socioeconomic challenge, an AI policy report that does not seriously address labour market disruption will lack credibility with policymakers.

**Suggestion:** Add a dedicated subsection (perhaps in Section 7 or as a new Section 5.6) analyzing AI's labour market implications for SACU, drawing on the emerging academic literature (e.g., ScienceDirect articles on AI and employment in South Africa) and PwC/EY projections.

### 7. Gender and inclusion dimensions are tokenistic

The report mentions "gender, linguistic, and socioeconomic biases" once (Section 7.5) and "gender inclusion" in passing (re: the Windhoek Statement). There is no substantive analysis of:

- Differential internet access by gender across SACU members
- Women's representation in AI workforces and decision-making
- How AI-driven automation may disproportionately affect sectors with high female employment (e.g., garment manufacturing in Lesotho, domestic work in South Africa)
- The intersection of gender with other axes of exclusion (rural, linguistic minority, disability)

The AU Continental AI Strategy explicitly emphasizes gender. A report that claims to align with the AU framework should do the same.

### 8. The private sector is a ghost in this report

The report is overwhelmingly focused on government policy. Yet in practice, AI deployment in SACU is driven by private firms -- banks, telecoms, mining companies, retailers, fintechs. The report mentions exactly one private-sector deployment (the Proto AICX chatbot, which it cannot even fully verify). There is no analysis of:

- Which companies are deploying AI in SACU and what they need from regulation
- How South African firms (MTN, Vodacom, Standard Bank, Discovery, Naspers/Prosus) are shaping AI adoption regionally
- The role of multinational tech firms (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Huawei) in the region
- How the private sector would respond to harmonization

Recommendations for regulatory sandboxes and mutual recognition of standards are hollow without understanding what the private sector is actually doing and what it needs.

### 9. The US/China tech competition dimension is missing

This is a first-order strategic issue for SACU that the report ignores entirely:

- China has pledged $10 billion to South Africa and is deeply involved in Africa's digital infrastructure (fiber optics, mobile towers, data centers). At the 2024 FOCAC Summit, 20 digital infrastructure projects were announced for 2025-2027.
- Huawei and ZTE are major providers of telecommunications infrastructure across SACU members.
- The US is increasingly framing AI governance as a geopolitical competition, with implications for SACU members who must navigate between the two.
- Africa's $2 billion surveillance technology purchases from Chinese firms raise governance questions directly relevant to AI regulation.

A SACU AI governance framework that does not acknowledge these geopolitical dynamics is incomplete. The choice of regulatory models (EU-aligned, US-aligned, or a third way) has geopolitical implications that the report should address.

### 10. Infrastructure constraints deserve more than a paragraph

The report correctly identifies electricity and connectivity as prerequisites for AI deployment but treats them superficially. Key gaps:

- South Africa's load-shedding crisis is mentioned once. No data on Eskom's AI-relevant capacity constraints.
- AI data center energy consumption is growing rapidly worldwide. What does this mean for a region with constrained power grids?
- Mobile-only internet access (the norm in smaller SACU members) fundamentally limits what AI applications are feasible. The report notes this but does not explore the implications for which AI applications are realistic versus aspirational.
- No discussion of the cost of connectivity relative to income, which is the binding constraint in Lesotho and Eswatini.

### 11. The ASEAN comparison is more useful than the EU comparison, but the report leads with the EU

Section 6.2 spends more space on the EU AI Act than on ASEAN. But ASEAN's soft-law, consensus-driven approach -- among members with vastly different income levels and institutional capacities -- is far more analogous to SACU's situation than the EU model. The EU has supranational institutions, a single market acquis, and decades of regulatory harmonization infrastructure that SACU simply does not have. Leading with the EU comparison risks setting unrealistic expectations.

**Suggestion:** Restructure Section 6 to lead with ASEAN and draw more granular lessons from it. Keep the EU as a longer-term aspiration but be explicit about why the EU model is not directly transferable.

### 12. The SARFAI/Windhoek Statement timeline is unclear

The report describes Namibia hosting "the first UNESCO-Southern Africa sub-Regional Forum on Artificial Intelligence (SARFAI)" without dating it. Web searches confirm this event took place in **September 2022** -- nearly four years ago. Presenting it without a date creates the misleading impression that it is recent. The report should also note what, if anything, has happened with the Windhoek Statement since 2022 -- has it been implemented? Has it generated follow-up action?

### 13. South-South learning opportunities are underexplored

The report benchmarks against the EU and ASEAN but ignores regional blocs with more comparable conditions:

- **The East African Community (EAC):** Kenya and Rwanda are significantly ahead of most SACU members on AI governance. What can SACU learn from them?
- **ECOWAS:** Has its own digital economy initiatives. Nigeria's AI ecosystem is larger than any SACU member except South Africa.
- **Mercosur:** A customs union with extreme asymmetry (Brazil vs. Paraguay) -- directly analogous to the SA vs. Lesotho dynamic.

## Minor Points (Nice-to-Have Improvements)

### 14. The "5.6% increase" framing (Section 2) is misleading

The report says AI could contribute "$1.2 trillion to Africa's GDP by 2030 -- a 5.6% increase over baseline projections." Even setting aside the attribution problem, presenting $1.2T as 5.6% of baseline implies Africa's baseline GDP is ~$21.4 trillion. Africa's current GDP is approximately $3.1 trillion. This arithmetic does not work. Either the percentage or the dollar figure is wrong, or the baseline is being defined in a non-obvious way.

### 15. The report does not discuss SACU's revenue-sharing formula implications

SACU's distinctive feature -- the Common Revenue Pool and revenue-sharing formula -- is unmentioned. If digital services increasingly substitute for physical goods subject to customs duties, this has direct implications for the revenue pool that funds the smaller members. This connection between digital transformation and SACU's fiscal architecture is a unique analytical contribution the report could make.

### 16. Recommendations lack cost estimates

None of the ten recommendations includes even a rough cost estimate. A SACU Digital Economy Working Group meeting quarterly across five countries: what does that cost? A regional regulatory sandbox: what is the price tag? Development partners and finance ministries will immediately ask these questions.

### 17. The "window for action is narrow" framing is overused

The conclusion states "The window for action is narrow" and lists several deadlines. But most of the cited deadlines (South Africa's AI policy finalization in 2027, AU Phase 1 through 2026) are not hard constraints -- they are aspirational timelines that are themselves likely to slip. Overstating urgency can undermine credibility.

### 18. The Namibia RAM launch date discrepancy

The report states the RAM report "was launched in August 2025." Web searches confirm the domestic launch was on **14 August 2025** in Windhoek, but the report was first presented at the **3rd UNESCO Global Forum on the Ethics of AI** in Bangkok in **June 2025**. This is minor but worth clarifying for precision.

### 19. Lesotho Data Protection Act dating

The report refers to the "Data Protection Act of 2011." Legal sources indicate this was **Act No. 5 of 2012**, published in the Government Gazette on 22 February 2012. The bill was passed in 2011, hence the common shorthand, but for a policy report citing legislation, the enacted date should be used.

### 20. The report could benefit from a risk matrix

A visual summary -- mapping each SACU member against AI governance dimensions (data protection, AI policy, institutional capacity, infrastructure) -- would make the comparative analysis more accessible to policymakers.

## Specific Text Suggestions

### 1. Executive Summary GDP share

**Current text:**
> "South Africa, which accounts for roughly 93% of the bloc's combined GDP"

**Suggested replacement:**
> "South Africa, which accounts for roughly 91% of the bloc's combined GDP"

*Rationale:* 93% contradicts the report's own data table ($426.4bn / $467.7bn = 91.2%).

---

### 2. McKinsey attribution

**Current text:**
> "McKinsey Global Institute estimates that AI could contribute up to $1.2 trillion to Africa's GDP by 2030 -- a 5.6% increase over baseline projections."

**Suggested replacement:**
> "Various estimates suggest AI could contribute significantly to Africa's GDP, with PwC projecting up to $1.2 trillion by 2030 and McKinsey estimating $61--103 billion annually from generative AI alone. For SACU, even conservative scenarios imply substantial potential gains -- provided the enabling conditions are in place."

*Rationale:* Correct attribution, acknowledge range, avoid false precision.

---

### 3. Oxford Insights framing

**Current text:**
> "Oxford AI Readiness Rank (2025)"

**Suggested replacement:**
> Verify whether the data is from the 2024 or 2025 edition and label accordingly. If the 2025 data shows a lower rank for South Africa than claimed, this weakens the "top 5 in Africa" narrative and should be acknowledged.

---

### 4. Unemployment reference

**Current text:**
> "with attention to South Africa's 32%+ unemployment rate"

**Suggested replacement:**
> "with attention to South Africa's official unemployment rate of 31.4% (Q4 2025), rising to 42.1% on the expanded definition that includes discouraged workers -- among the highest rates globally"

*Rationale:* More precise, more impactful, properly dated.

---

### 5. Botswana GDP contraction

**Current text:**
> "with the IMF projecting a GDP contraction of 0.4% in 2025"

**Suggested replacement:**
> "with the IMF projecting a GDP contraction of 1.0% in 2025, following a 3.0% contraction in 2024, driven by a 24% decline in mining output and 34% slump in diamond trading"

*Rationale:* Uses latest IMF Article IV data and conveys the severity more accurately.

---

### 6. Costs of inaction GDP extrapolation

**Current text:**
> "SACU's share -- roughly 15% of Africa's GDP -- implies a potential gain on the order of $50-100 billion over the coming decade."

**Suggested replacement:**
> "While precise estimates are difficult, SACU accounts for roughly 15% of Africa's GDP. If the continent captures even a fraction of the AI gains projected by various studies, the opportunity cost of regulatory fragmentation -- even measured in delayed adoption of a few years -- would be significant."

*Rationale:* The original extrapolation (15% of a misattributed $1.2T = $180B, then arbitrarily halved to "$50-100B") has no methodological basis. The replacement preserves the directional argument without false precision.

---

### 7. SARFAI contextual dating

**Current text:**
> "Namibia hosted the first UNESCO-Southern Africa sub-Regional Forum on Artificial Intelligence (SARFAI), bringing together ministers from Botswana, Malawi, Mozambique, South Africa, Zambia, and Zimbabwe."

**Suggested replacement:**
> "In September 2022, Namibia hosted the first UNESCO-Southern Africa sub-Regional Forum on Artificial Intelligence (SARFAI), bringing together ministers from Botswana, Malawi, Mozambique, South Africa, Zambia, and Zimbabwe. The resulting Windhoek Statement called for regional AI coordination, though follow-up action has been limited."

*Rationale:* Without the date, readers may assume this was recent. Adding the implementation note provides honest context.

---

## Summary of Recommended Priorities

1. **Fix the Oxford Insights data** -- this affects the entire analytical framework and a prominent data table.
2. **Correct the McKinsey attribution** -- this is cited in two key sections and underpins the economic case.
3. **Add labour market analysis** -- this is the single largest substantive gap.
4. **Add geopolitical context (US/China)** -- essential for any AI governance report about Africa in 2026.
5. **Add cost estimates to recommendations** -- transforms them from aspirational to actionable.
6. **Update Botswana GDP and SA unemployment figures** -- straightforward fixes that improve credibility.
