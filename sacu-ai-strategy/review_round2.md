# Review: AI Strategy and Regulation in the SACU Region (Round 2)

**Reviewer:** Good-faith critical review
**Date:** 25 March 2026
**Document reviewed:** draft.md (revised following Round 1 feedback)

---

## Executive Assessment

This is now a substantially stronger report. The Round 2 revision has addressed the most important factual errors from Round 1 (McKinsey attribution, GDP share inconsistency, Botswana contraction, unemployment figures) and has added significant new content on labour markets, gender, geopolitics, and infrastructure. The addition of cost estimates to the recommendations is a meaningful improvement. The report now engages with the issues that matter most to policymakers in the region.

That said, the revision has introduced a new structural problem: the report has grown from what was a focused policy assessment into something closer to a comprehensive survey, and the new sections -- while individually competent -- are not yet fully integrated into the analytical narrative. The report reads, at points, as though Sections 6-10 were bolted onto a pre-existing Sections 1-5 structure. The recommendations, while improved, still do not adequately grapple with the most challenging trade-offs the report itself identifies. And there are several important recent publications -- notably the World Bank's Digital Progress and Trends Report 2025, the UNCTAD Technology and Innovation Report 2025, the BRICS AI Governance Declaration (July 2025), and the Carnegie Endowment's September 2025 analysis of Africa's AI governance landscape -- that the report should engage with.

**Overall verdict:** The report is approaching publication-readiness but needs one more round of structural tightening, better integration of new sections, and engagement with the most important counterarguments and recent literature.

## New Issues

### A. The 2025 Oxford Insights Index Is Now Available

The report uses the 2024 Oxford Insights Government AI Readiness Index, which is defensible as of the writing date. However, the 2025 edition was published in January 2026 and is now publicly available. Key findings from the 2025 edition that are relevant to the report:

- **South Africa ranks 67th globally** -- not the "leading African nation" in the broader sense. Kenya (65th) and Mauritius (71st, but leading in SSA by score at 53.27) both feature prominently.
- South Africa leads SSA in the Data & Infrastructure and Technology Sector pillars, and is the only SSA country scoring above the global average for the Technology Sector pillar.
- **No SSA country scores above 56/100 on AI infrastructure**, compared with the US at 89.27 and China at 76.92.
- The East/Southern African cluster (Kenya 65th, SA 67th, Rwanda 75th) is noteworthy for regional coordination discussions.

The report should at minimum note that the 2025 edition exists and that South Africa's position has shifted. The current framing of SA as "leading African nation" is accurate for the 2024 edition but no longer holds in the 2025 edition (Kenya ranks higher overall). This matters because it affects the narrative about SA's anchor role.

**Action required:** Add a footnote or parenthetical noting the 2025 index results, or update the table to reference both editions. At minimum, qualify the "leading African nation" claim.

### B. The Report Does Not Engage with the World Bank's "4Cs" Framework

The World Bank's *Digital Progress and Trends Report 2025: Strengthening AI Foundations* (November 2025) introduces a "4Cs" framework for AI readiness in developing countries: **Connectivity, Compute, Context (locally relevant data), and Competency (digital skills)**. This framework is directly relevant to the report's analysis in Sections 5 and 10, and is likely to become the dominant analytical lens for AI readiness in development policy discussions. The report's Section 5 ("What Good Looks Like") covers similar ground but does not reference this framework. Aligning with or at least acknowledging the 4Cs would strengthen the report's positioning within the broader policy literature.

The World Bank is also preparing the **World Development Report 2026: Artificial Intelligence for Development**, which will be the institution's flagship statement on AI and development. The report should flag this upcoming publication as context for its own recommendations.

**Action required:** Reference the 4Cs framework in Section 5 or Section 10. Note the forthcoming WDR 2026.

### C. The UNCTAD Technology and Innovation Report 2025 Is Missing

UNCTAD's *Technology and Innovation Report 2025: Inclusive Artificial Intelligence for Development* (published April 2025) provides critical context:

- The global AI market is projected to reach **$4.8 trillion by 2033**.
- **118 countries -- mostly in the Global South -- are absent from major AI governance discussions**.
- Under one-third of developing countries have AI strategies.
- **2.6 billion people remain offline** globally.
- Just 100 companies (mostly US and China) account for 40% of global private R&D investment.

The 118-country exclusion statistic is particularly powerful for the report's argument about the costs of inaction and the value of regional coordination. The $4.8 trillion market projection is a useful complement to the AfDB and McKinsey figures already cited.

**Action required:** Add UNCTAD TIR 2025 findings to Section 2 (problem statement) and Section 11 (costs of inaction).

### D. The BRICS AI Governance Declaration Is Relevant but Unmentioned

South Africa is a BRICS member. The **BRICS Leaders' Statement on the Global Governance of Artificial Intelligence** (July 2025, adopted at the 17th BRICS Summit in Rio de Janeiro) is directly relevant:

- It emphasizes **digital sovereignty** -- each nation's right to establish its own AI regulatory frameworks.
- It advocates for **UN-centered AI governance** rather than club-based models.
- It calls for **fair data governance** enabling developing countries to benefit from their own data.
- It addresses **market concentration** in AI technologies.

South Africa's commitment to the BRICS AI governance position has implications for how SACU approaches the geopolitical dimensions discussed in Section 8. The "third way" framing in Section 8.3 would benefit from referencing the BRICS position, which provides institutional backing for exactly this approach.

**Action required:** Add a paragraph in Section 8 referencing the BRICS AI Governance Declaration as context for South Africa's geopolitical positioning.

### E. The Private Sector Gap Remains the Biggest Substantive Weakness

The R1 review flagged this and the editor's notes acknowledge it as "partially addressed." It is not. The geopolitics section discusses Chinese multinational infrastructure firms, but the report still says almost nothing about:

- **South African firms deploying AI**: Standard Bank, Discovery, Vodacom, MTN, Naspers/Prosus, Capitec, Shoprite -- these companies are making AI deployment decisions now, and their cross-border operations within SACU mean their practices effectively set de facto regional standards.
- **What the private sector needs from regulation**: Certainty? Flexibility? Sandboxes? The report recommends sandboxes and mutual recognition but without any evidence about whether firms actually want these things.
- **The fintech ecosystem**: The report mentions Eswatini's fintech strategy but does not discuss the broader fintech ecosystem across SACU, which is where many AI deployments are actually happening.

The Carnegie Endowment's September 2025 analysis of Africa's AI governance landscape emphasizes that Africa has published 15 national AI strategies, but the critical gap is in implementation and private-sector engagement. The report's recommendations risk the same disconnect.

**Action required:** Even a half-page subsection in the case for coordination (Section 9) or the recommendations (Section 12) discussing what the private sector is actually doing and what it needs from a SACU framework would substantially strengthen the report's credibility.

### F. The Report Does Not Acknowledge the Regulation-Innovation Trade-Off

The report argues consistently for more regulation and coordination, which is a defensible position. But it does not acknowledge the strongest counterargument: that premature or heavy-handed regulation can stifle innovation, particularly in developing countries where the AI ecosystem is nascent.

The evidence on this trade-off is relevant and recent:

- The EU has already acknowledged that the AI Act may be deterring investment. The European Commission's **Digital Omnibus package** (November 2025) aims to simplify AI Act compliance, and there is active discussion about delaying high-risk system obligations by one year amid industry pressure.
- Business leaders have raised concerns about the EU AI Act causing companies to adopt a "reticent approach to deploying AI more widely throughout the European Union."
- By early 2026, **over 72 countries have launched more than 1,000 AI policy initiatives**, with scope varying "dramatically from binding legislation with heavy penalties to voluntary guidelines with no enforcement mechanism." This diversity of approaches is itself evidence that there is no consensus on the optimal regulatory stance.

For SACU, where AI ecosystems are far less developed than in the EU, the risk of over-regulation is arguably higher. The report should explicitly address this: why is the coordination it recommends unlikely to stifle innovation? The ASEAN soft-law model provides part of the answer, but the report should state the trade-off and explain why its recommendations navigate it appropriately.

**Action required:** Add a paragraph (in Section 9 or Section 12) acknowledging the regulation-innovation trade-off and explaining why the proposed approach -- starting with non-binding principles and building toward binding rules -- is designed to avoid the EU's emerging difficulties.

### G. The AU Strategy Implementation Update Is Missing

The report references the AU Continental AI Strategy (July 2024) and its Phase 1 (2025-2026) implementation timeline. But as of early 2026, there is more to say:

- A **High-Level Policy Dialogue on AI** in May 2025 produced a communique declaring AI a "strategic priority" for Africa.
- Sixteen months into implementation, early indicators show "unprecedented momentum -- over $100 million in partnerships, 250,000+ people trained, and multiple countries launching aligned national strategies."
- The **Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence** has been endorsed by 49 countries.
- A proposed **$60 billion Africa AI Fund** and **Africa AI Council** are under discussion.

These developments are directly relevant to Section 9.4 and the conclusion. They suggest the continental framework is more active than the report implies.

**Action required:** Update Section 9.4 with the latest AU implementation progress.

## Literature Comparisons

### How Does This Report Compare to Recent Work?

**World Bank (Digital Progress and Trends Report 2025):** The World Bank's 4Cs framework (Connectivity, Compute, Context, Competency) provides a more structured analytical lens than the report's Section 5 benchmarks. The report covers similar ground but in a less systematic way. The World Bank also emphasizes that "successful AI adoption in developing countries depends less on technology itself than on the surrounding ecosystem -- skills, data, finance, regulation and trust." The SACU report would benefit from this framing.

**UNCTAD (Technology and Innovation Report 2025):** UNCTAD's finding that 118 countries are excluded from AI governance discussions powerfully reinforces the report's argument for regional coordination. The $4.8 trillion market projection provides a useful framing. The UNCTAD emphasis on AI's impact on 40% of jobs globally is consistent with the report's labour market section but provides a global benchmark.

**Carnegie Endowment (September 2025):** The Carnegie analysis identifies five critical enablers for AI in Africa -- talent, data, governance, research/innovation, and digital infrastructure -- and emphasizes that the gap is in implementation rather than strategy. The finding that Africa has published 15 national AI strategies is relevant context for the report's discussion of SACU members' policy efforts.

**BRICS (July 2025):** The BRICS AI Governance Declaration's emphasis on digital sovereignty, UN-centered governance, and fair data access aligns with the report's "third way" argument in Section 8 but provides institutional backing that the report does not reference.

**ASEAN (2025-2026 updates):** The report correctly identifies ASEAN as the most relevant comparator. However, ASEAN has moved faster than the report implies: Vietnam became the first Southeast Asian country to promulgate an AI Law (December 2025, effective March 2026), Indonesia is expected to sign off on AI regulations in early 2026, and an ASEAN Foundation report found a "consistent gap between high levels of AI usage and actual readiness." The ASEAN trajectory -- from soft law (2024) toward binding national rules (2025-2026) -- provides a real-time case study of the path the report recommends for SACU.

**EAC (2025-2026):** Kenya's National AI Strategy 2025-2030 (launched March 2025) is the most advanced national AI strategy in East/Southern Africa. Rwanda's 2023 AI Policy positioned it as an early mover. The EAC is advancing a Regional Artificial Intelligence Strategy, with a major conference held in Kigali in March 2026. These are directly relevant comparators that the report omits entirely (as flagged in R1 Issue #13).

## Structural Suggestions

### 1. The Report Needs a Tighter Narrative Thread

The original report (Sections 1-5, 9, 11-13 in the current numbering) had a clear through-line: fragmented regulation across SACU --> comparative benchmarks --> case for coordination --> costs of inaction --> recommendations. The new sections (6: Labour, 7: Gender, 8: Geopolitics, 10: Infrastructure) are substantively strong but interrupt this flow. They feel like they were inserted into the middle of an existing argument rather than woven into it.

**Suggestion:** Consider restructuring as follows:
- Sections 1-4 (as now): context and country analysis.
- Fold Section 5 (benchmarks), Section 10 (infrastructure), and the 4Cs framework into a single "Enabling Conditions and Benchmarks" section.
- Fold Section 6 (labour) and Section 7 (gender) into a single "Socioeconomic Implications" section, positioned after the country analysis and before the case for coordination.
- Move Section 8 (geopolitics) to follow the case for coordination, as it provides context for the "what kind of framework" question.
- This produces a flow: Context --> Countries --> Enabling Conditions --> Socioeconomic Stakes --> Case for Coordination --> Geopolitical Context --> Costs of Inaction --> Recommendations.

### 2. Section Weighting Is Uneven

The country analysis (Section 4) accounts for roughly 40% of the report's length, which is appropriate given that it is the report's strongest section. But Section 7 (Gender) is noticeably shorter and thinner than Section 6 (Labour) or Section 8 (Geopolitics). If gender is important enough to merit its own section, it needs to be as substantive as the others. If it cannot be fleshed out further, it would be better integrated into the labour market or socioeconomic implications section.

### 3. The Recommendations Need a "Theory of Change"

The recommendations are well-sequenced (immediate/medium/longer-term) and now include cost estimates, which is good. But they lack a theory of change: what is the political economy that will make these happen? Who are the champions? What are the incentives for member states to participate? The report notes that "political will" is missing but does not analyze why or how to generate it.

Key questions the recommendations should address:
- **Why would South Africa participate?** SA could regulate unilaterally and let its market power set de facto regional standards. What does it gain from a SACU-level process?
- **Who pays?** The cost estimates are useful but the funding sources are vague ("development partners," "bilateral donors"). Which partners? Are they already engaged?
- **What happens if one member defects?** If Lesotho does not update its data protection law, do the other four proceed without it?

### 3. Missing Regulation-Innovation Caveat

**Suggested addition at the end of Section 9.2 (after the ASEAN discussion):**

> A critical design consideration is the risk that premature regulation stifles the innovation it seeks to govern. The EU's experience is instructive: less than two years after the AI Act's entry into force, the European Commission has introduced a Digital Omnibus package (November 2025) to simplify compliance, amid evidence that regulatory burdens are deterring AI investment and deployment. For SACU, where AI ecosystems are far less developed, this risk is arguably higher. The ASEAN-style approach recommended here -- beginning with non-binding principles and building toward binding rules as capacity and experience develop -- is specifically designed to avoid this trap. The goal is to establish a common floor of governance without creating a ceiling on innovation.

### 4. BRICS Reference in Geopolitics Section

**Suggested addition at the end of Section 8.3:**

> South Africa's membership in BRICS provides additional institutional context for this approach. The BRICS Leaders' Statement on the Global Governance of Artificial Intelligence (July 2025) explicitly endorses digital sovereignty, UN-centered AI governance, and fair data access for developing countries -- principles that align with the "strategic non-alignment" position outlined above and that carry the weight of a major multilateral grouping.

### 5. EAC Comparator

**Suggested addition in Section 9 (after Section 9.2 on ASEAN or as a new 9.X):**

> **The East African Community:** Within Africa, the EAC provides a closer comparator. Kenya's National AI Strategy 2025-2030 (launched March 2025) is the most comprehensive national AI governance document on the continent, covering infrastructure, data, governance, talent, and ethics. Rwanda's 2023 AI Policy positioned it as an early mover. The EAC is advancing a Regional Artificial Intelligence Strategy, with a major conference hosted in Kigali in March 2026. Kenya and South Africa rank closely on the 2025 Oxford Insights index (65th and 67th respectively), suggesting comparable readiness levels. SACU could benefit from structured engagement with EAC experience -- particularly Kenya's approach to regulatory sandboxes and Rwanda's positioning as an "AI lab" for Africa.

### 6. Costs of Inaction -- UNCTAD Data

**Suggested addition in Section 11.1:**

UNCTAD's Technology and Innovation Report 2025 projects the global AI market will reach $4.8 trillion by 2033 -- roughly the size of Germany's current economy. Yet 118 countries, mostly in the Global South, remain absent from major AI governance discussions. Under one-third of developing countries have AI strategies. For SACU, where only South Africa has a comprehensive AI policy in development, the risk of being excluded from both the economic gains and the governance conversations is real.

## Summary of Recommended Priorities for Round 3

1. **Update Oxford Insights references** to acknowledge the 2025 edition (South Africa 67th, Kenya ahead). This affects the narrative about SA's regional anchor role.
2. **Add the regulation-innovation trade-off** as an explicit caveat. Without it, the report reads as pure advocacy.
3. **Reference the World Bank 4Cs framework and UNCTAD TIR 2025** -- these are the publications that will define the policy conversation the report is entering.
4. **Add the BRICS AI Governance Declaration** to the geopolitics section.
5. **Structural tightening** -- either integrate the new sections more seamlessly or restructure to create a clearer narrative flow.
6. **Add even a brief private-sector paragraph** -- the report cannot credibly recommend regulatory sandboxes without discussing who would use them.
7. **Add an EAC comparator** -- Kenya and Rwanda are the most relevant African benchmarks and their omission is conspicuous.
