## Top 3 Priorities for Final Round

### 1. The Conclusion's Final Sentence Still Reads as Advocacy

The report's concluding sentence -- "that opportunity requires action, not further deliberation" -- is an advocacy statement, not an analytical finding. The rest of the conclusion has improved substantially: it summarizes evidence, acknowledges trade-offs, and references the theory of change. But this final line undermines the balanced analytical tone the report has otherwise achieved.

**Suggested fix:** Replace with something that restates the analytical conclusion rather than issuing a call to action. For example: "Whether SACU seizes that opportunity will depend on the political choices its members make in the next two to three years -- choices that the converging policy timelines identified in this report make both possible and, from an economic standpoint, advisable."

### 2. The SACU Revenue-Sharing Implication Deserves at Least a Paragraph

The editor's notes acknowledge this gap and defer it to a "supplementary note." This is the third consecutive round in which this has been flagged. While the report cannot be expected to do full fiscal modelling, the absence of even a qualitative discussion is a missed opportunity for a distinctive analytical contribution.

The core issue is straightforward: SACU's revenue-sharing formula allocates customs revenue based on intra-SACU trade in physical goods. As digital services (including AI-powered services) substitute for physical goods, the customs revenue base erodes -- disproportionately affecting the smaller members (Botswana, Namibia, Eswatini, Lesotho) for whom SACU revenue transfers are a significant share of fiscal income. Botswana, Namibia, and Lesotho derive roughly 25-40% of government revenue from the SACU Common Revenue Pool. This creates a structural fiscal incentive for the smaller members to engage with digital economy governance -- which is, in fact, an argument *for* the coordination the report recommends.

**Suggested fix:** Add a short paragraph in Section 7.1 (Why SACU-Level Coordination Matters) or Section 9.1 (Economic Costs) noting that the substitution of digital for physical trade has implications for the SACU revenue-sharing formula, and that this creates an additional fiscal incentive for smaller members to participate in regional digital governance. No modelling required -- just economic reasoning.

### 3. The SA AI Policy Gazetting Is Happening Now -- The Report Should Note This

The report states that the Draft National AI Policy "is expected to be gazetted for a 60-day public consultation period in March 2026" (line 121). Given that the report itself is dated March 2026, and multiple legal sources (Baker McKenzie, Fasken, ITWeb) confirm that gazetting is imminent or may have already occurred by the time this is published, the report should either:

(a) Update the language to reflect the most current status at publication time (e.g., "has been gazetted" or "was gazetted in March 2026" if confirmed), or
(b) Add a footnote acknowledging that this is a rapidly moving situation and readers should check the DCDT website for the latest status.

This matters because the SA gazetting is the single most significant near-term development for SACU AI governance. Getting it right signals that the report is current.

## Minor Polish Items

1. **Population figure inconsistency.** The table (line 93) gives SACU total population as "~73.8 million." Line 74 (Introduction) says "73 million." Line 453 (Section 7.4) says "74 million." These should be standardized -- "approximately 74 million" throughout would be simplest and consistent with the table sum.

2. **AU Strategy timeline.** Section 7.6 says "Sixteen months into implementation" of the AU Continental AI Strategy (endorsed July 2024). By March 2026, that is closer to 20 months. If the "sixteen months" figure comes from a source published in late 2025, note the source date; otherwise update to ~20 months.

3. **Section numbering in text vs. Table of Contents.** The ToC lists Sections 1-13 (with Section 12 as References and 13 as Editor's Notes). The body uses Sections 1-11 for substantive content, with 12 (References) and 13 (Editor's Notes). This is fine, but the Recommendations section is numbered 10 in the body. Verify that all internal cross-references (if any) match the final numbering.

4. **"Social stability imperative" (line 650).** This phrase in the conclusion -- "getting AI governance right is not merely a technology policy question -- it is a social stability imperative" -- is strong language. It is defensible given the unemployment data, but "imperative" carries normative weight. Consider whether "concern" or "challenge" better fits the analytical register.

5. **Repetition of UNCTAD statistics.** The $4.8 trillion and 118-country figures now appear in: (a) Executive Summary, (b) Introduction, (c) Costs of Inaction, and (d) Conclusion. This is two too many. The Executive Summary and one body section (Costs of Inaction, where they do the most analytical work) would be sufficient. The Introduction and Conclusion could reference the findings without repeating the exact numbers.

6. **Private-sector section placement.** Section 6.5 (private sector) sits within "Socioeconomic Implications: Labour Markets, Gender, and Inclusion." The private-sector content is relevant to the socioeconomic discussion but is also analytically distinct -- it is about what firms need from regulation, not about labour market or gender impacts. Consider whether a sentence in the section introduction could frame the private-sector subsection as bridging the socioeconomic analysis and the case for coordination that follows.

## Overall Verdict

The report is ready for publication with minor edits. The three priorities above are improvements, not blockers. The structural restructure has been executed well, the new content is integrated rather than bolted on, and the theory of change gives the recommendations political credibility they previously lacked. The regulation-innovation trade-off section (7.5) and the private-sector subsection (6.5) are the two additions that most improve the report's standing as balanced policy analysis.

The SACU revenue-sharing point remains a missed opportunity but is not essential to the report's core argument. If it cannot be addressed in the final round, a one-sentence acknowledgment that this is an area for further analysis would be sufficient.
